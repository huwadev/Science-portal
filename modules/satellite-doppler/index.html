Radio Doppler Shift & LEO Tracking

Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites travel at extremely high relative velocities ($\approx 7.5\text{ km/s}$ at $\approx 600\text{ km}$ altitude) to counteract gravity. Due to the **Doppler Effect**, electromagnetic waves emitted by an approaching satellite appear compressed (frequency shifted upward), while waves from a receding satellite appear stretched (frequency shifted downward). Radio transceivers must adjust their tuning in real time to match this S-curve frequency drift, which sweeps steepest when the satellite reaches its **Closest Approach (TCA)** and the range rate swings from negative to positive.
